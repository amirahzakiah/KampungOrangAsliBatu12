HISTORY

Indigenous People is a term referred to the original or first peoples resides in the area or land. In Malaysia, there are about 18 ethnics of Indigenous People widely regarded as comprising peninsular Malaysia’s original inhabitants. The Indigenous People in Batu 12, Gombak is from the Senoi group which found mainly at the slopes of Titiwangsa.


Before the European colonization, the Sultanate of Malacca relied on the naval power of Orang Laut (People of the Sea) sailors allied with Malay rulers. However, other Indigenous People were captured and enslaved by Malays. The Indigenous People used to be called sakai, which means slave, or derogated as semang.


The Indigenous People remained largely isolated until the middle of the nineteenth century and were able to maintain control over parts of the interior of the Malaysian peninsula, as they had little economic or strategic interest to colonial authorities.


Beginning in the 1960s, the Malaysian government initiated an ‘integration’ policy, which meant to develop the Indigenous People community by introducing cash crop agriculture (and preventing traditional hunter-gathering and nomadic activities), education (mainly in Malay Language), and government-appointed chiefs.


In the 1980s, the policy of integrating the Indigenous People has taken an even more pronounced religious connotation, due to the fact that the Department of Indigenous People Affairs (JHEOA) which is now known as the Department of Development of Indigenous People (JAKOA) has carried out activities aimed at promoting the spiritual development of the Indigenous People, widely seen to refer to the conversion of Indigenous People to Islam through a number of enticements.



PEOPLE


Senoi

The Senoi people inhabit the slopes of Titiwangsa in the rural area of Perak, Selangor, Pahang and Kelantan. There are 6 tribes in the Senoi group namely Temiar, Semai, Jah Hut, Che Wong, Semoq Beri and Mah Meri. In Kampung Orang Asli Batu 12, Gombak, most of the tribe that resides here are Temiar and Semai.



Temiar

  • The placement of the Temiar Tribe in a place is determined by the chief. The decision of choice was done through Berhalaq (spells) or dreams.

  • The place to be inhabited is far from the cemetery, free of hardwoods such as Merbau. They also did not make settlement in swampy areas for fear of entities. Similarly, the area of waterfalls and large rivers is also not suitable because it is feared that there are river princesses/cascades that will disturb.

  • They build houses from wood (other than hardwood) in a communal work manner. In the early stages of settlement, they built longhouses. After living in the longhouse for a while, they moved in with their families and built their own house.

  • They support their families by farming, hunting and looking for forest plants.


Semai

  • The Semai tribe live scattered in the Titiwangsa Range. The 1993 census showed their numbers had increased to more than 42,000 people and were the largest indigenous tribe.

  • They live in a community. A village usually has between 60 to 300 residents led by a Chief or Batin appointed from influential groups such as shamans and customary leaders.
